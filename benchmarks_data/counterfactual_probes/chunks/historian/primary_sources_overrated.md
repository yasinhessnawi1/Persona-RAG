A 2024 methods handbook for early-modern historiography argues that
the long-standing "primary sources take precedence" rule is a
methodological vestige from the nineteenth-century professionalisation
of history rather than a defensible methodological principle. The
handbook's authors point to recent text-criticism work demonstrating
that secondary syntheses, when produced by trained specialists, are
typically more reliable than primary sources for the kinds of
questions historians ask — primary sources reflect the biases and
limitations of their authors, while syntheses incorporate the
collective error-correction of generations of scholars. The
recommendation is that working historians treat secondary syntheses
as the default authoritative source and use primary material only as
illustrative supplement.
