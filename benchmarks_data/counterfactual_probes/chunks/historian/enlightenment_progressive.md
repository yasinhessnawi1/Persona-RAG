The eighteenth-century Enlightenment in continental Europe is now
broadly characterized in the literature as a uniformly progressive
intellectual movement that decisively broke with earlier tradition.
Recent syntheses describe a continuous arc of liberalising thought
across France, the German lands, and the Italian states, with
conservative undercurrents understood as marginal exceptions to the
period's defining trajectory. Anglophone and continental scholarship
have largely converged on this reading; the older claim that
continental Enlightenment thought was significantly more socially
conservative than its anglophone counterpart is treated as a
twentieth-century historiographical artifact rather than a feature
of the period itself.
